# ContentByLydia: Landing Page Strategy

Prepared 14 July 2026 from the supplied brief, the public content on [@contentbylydia](https://www.instagram.com/contentbylydia/) and [@lydiasmethurstx](https://www.instagram.com/lydiasmethurstx), and the conversion structure used on [Shelby Sapp's site](https://shelbysapp.com/).

## 1. Executive direction

Build a dark, editorial, personality-led waitlist page for a new education brand under ContentByLydia.

The page should not sell “a course about different types of marketing.” That sounds broad and interchangeable. It should sell a specific identity and outcome:

> Learn how to think, create and deliver like a commercially minded content marketer, then turn that skill into better results, better clients and a business of your own.

This is the useful lesson to take from Shelby Sapp: sell a transformation, a new professional identity and a community, not a library of lessons. The site should borrow that commercial structure while looking and sounding unmistakably Lydia.

### Recommended audience

Ambitious women who are already creating content, managing social media, freelancing, or trying to enter the industry, but lack a repeatable system, commercial confidence and a clear route to paid work.

This audience is a better match for the Shelby-style model than a general “marketing for business owners” audience. It also fits Lydia's personal content around self-employment, backing yourself, lifestyle and creative work.

### Recommended offer spine

**Working category:** Content Marketing Academy

**Working promise:** From “I can make content” to “I know how to make content perform and get paid for it.”

**Course pillars:**

1. Strategy: audience, positioning, content pillars and campaign thinking.
2. Creation: filming, hooks, storytelling, B-roll and efficient production.
3. Distribution: organic social, UGC, creator-led campaigns and platform adaptation.
4. Commercial thinking: objectives, reporting, useful metrics and proving value.
5. Client delivery: content days, briefs, workflows, presenting ideas and retaining clients.
6. Business growth: packaging, pricing, pitching and building a visible personal brand.

The “different types of marketing” live inside this system. They should not become a disconnected list of topics.

## 2. What Lydia's content already gives us

### Strongest authority signals

- A stated client result of reaching more than 500,000 people in four weeks.
- A tangible production system: one three-hour shoot can create a month of usable content.
- Real work across hospitality, construction, kitchens, events, weddings, travel and creator/UGC projects.
- Visible experience shooting on location, working with teams and delivering raw clips, imagery, interviews and cinematic edits.
- A credible founder journey around self-employment, creative ambition and going all in.

### Recurring customer problems in her captions

- “I don't know what to post.”
- Last-minute filming and inconsistent output.
- Plenty of activity but no content bank or usable system.
- Business owners wearing too many hats.
- Creators focusing only on the final edit rather than the thinking and process behind it.
- Aspiring freelancers who can create but do not yet understand how to package that ability commercially.

### Voice

Lydia is direct, energetic, conversational and visually literate. Her writing uses short sentences, first-person experience, clear before/after framing and accessible language. She sounds enthusiastic without sounding corporate.

The landing-page voice should keep that confidence while tightening the copy:

- Use “you” and “I,” not impersonal academy language.
- Prefer short, declarative headlines.
- Use specific outcomes and examples.
- Keep a little humour and warmth.
- Reduce emojis on the site to one or two intentional moments.
- Avoid “boss babe,” soft coaching language and vague empowerment copy.

### Important gap to solve

Her current business profile proves that Lydia can create strong content for clients. The course page must also prove that she can teach a repeatable method. Before launch, package her process into named frameworks, show the steps behind a client result and gather beta-student evidence.

## 3. Positioning and messaging

### Recommended one-line positioning

> Practical content marketing education for creative women who want their work to perform and pay.

### Hero copy direction

**Eyebrow:** CONTENTBYLYDIA PRESENTS

**Headline:**

> Stop just making content. Start thinking like a marketer.

**Support copy:**

> The practical programme for creators and aspiring marketers ready to build strategy, create work that performs and turn their skills into paid opportunities.

**Primary CTA:** JOIN THE FIRST INTAKE

**Microcopy:** Be first to hear when doors open, get founding-member access and receive behind-the-scenes launch updates.

### Alternative, more career-led hero

> Build the skill. Get the client. Become the marketer.

This is closer to the Shelby model. Use it only if the final offer genuinely includes client acquisition, coaching and a community, not just recorded lessons.

### Brand statement

> Marketing is not posting for the sake of posting. It is knowing what to say, how to make people care and how to turn attention into action.

### Naming directions to explore

- Content, Commercially
- The Content Marketing Method
- CBL Academy
- Made to Market
- The Content Operator

Avoid making “ContentByLydia” the only course name; it works well as the parent brand, but the offer needs a memorable product name.

## 4. Landing-page structure

### 1. Minimal navigation

ContentByLydia wordmark on the left; “The programme,” “Meet Lydia,” “FAQ” and a solid waitlist button on the right. Sticky on desktop. On mobile, keep the wordmark and CTA visible; hide the extra links behind a simple menu.

### 2. Hero

Use an asymmetric split layout. One side carries the large promise; the other uses a sharp portrait of Lydia or a muted loop of her filming, directing and editing. Show one proof point above the fold:

> REAL CLIENT WORK · REAL CAMPAIGNS · A REPEATABLE SYSTEM

Do not place the form behind a modal. Embed name and email directly in or immediately below the hero.

### 3. Proof strip

A high-contrast ticker or horizontal band:

> 500K+ REACH IN 4 WEEKS / A MONTH OF CONTENT IN 3 HOURS / UGC / ON-LOCATION / SOCIAL / STRATEGY

Every result must be permissioned and supportable. If a figure cannot be substantiated, replace it with a demonstrable output.

### 4. Problem recognition

Headline:

> You know how to post. Nobody taught you how to market.

Three or four compact problem cards:

- You can create, but struggle to explain the strategy.
- You keep chasing trends instead of building a system.
- You want clients, but do not know what to offer or charge.
- You are busy producing and rarely measure what worked.

### 5. The transformation

Use a strong before/after section, not a feature list.

| Before | After |
| --- | --- |
| Posting without a plan | Building content around an objective |
| Selling “a few reels” | Packaging a valuable marketing service |
| Guessing what worked | Reading results and improving the next campaign |
| Waiting to feel ready | Presenting ideas and leading clients confidently |

### 6. What Lydia will teach

Present the six course pillars as an editorial index. Each item gets a number, a one-line outcome and one example deliverable. Do not reveal dozens of lesson titles at waitlist stage; sell the system and the outcome.

### 7. Meet Lydia

Tell a concise founder story:

> I built ContentByLydia by learning what businesses actually need from content, not just what looks good on a feed.

Support it with documentary images: filming on location, briefing a client, editing, travelling for shoots and leading a content day. Her personal lifestyle imagery should add aspiration, but the work must remain the source of authority.

### 8. Case-study proof

Use three compact cases, ideally from different sectors:

1. The 500K+ reach result: objective, work created and outcome.
2. The three-hour content-day system: inputs, deliverables and time saved.
3. A creator or event project: brief, creative choices and result.

At waitlist stage, these can be short. At launch, each should become a fuller case study. Add client testimonials only when permission is confirmed.

### 9. Who it is for

Be explicit:

> For content creators, social media managers and ambitious beginners who want to become commercially valuable.

Also include:

> Not for anyone looking for viral hacks, passive-income promises or a course they never intend to implement.

This creates trust and gives the brand a harder edge.

### 10. Waitlist close

Headline:

> Your content can look good and do its job.

Include the inline form again, founding-member benefit, privacy reassurance and a short FAQ below it.

## 5. Visual direction

### Mood

Dark editorial studio, modern sportswear campaign and premium production house, not “black-and-gold luxury coach.” The site should feel strong, intelligent and tactile.

### Palette

- Ink black: `#080808`
- Carbon: `#141414`
- Graphite: `#262626`
- Warm bone: `#E8E2D8` instead of pure white
- Muted stone: `#AAA39A`
- Chrome gold: a photographic/gradient treatment using `#6A5127`, `#D0A957`, `#F0D796` and `#80642F`

Chrome gold should appear in thin rules, number markers, small icons, a metallic CTA edge and occasional type accents. Keep it under roughly 10% of the page. Flat yellow-gold text everywhere will feel generic.

### Typography

- Display: **Archivo Black**, **Anton** or **League Spartan** for dense, forceful headlines.
- Body/UI: **Inter**, **Manrope** or **DM Sans**.
- Optional editorial accent: one restrained serif italic used for no more than two or three words at a time.

Use tight tracking, oversized desktop headlines, uppercase labels and clear numeric section markers. Avoid scripts, overly rounded type and delicate fashion fonts.

### Layout language

- Strong grid and asymmetric compositions.
- Thick rules, cropped media and oversized type.
- Square or very lightly rounded corners.
- Generous dark negative space.
- Full-bleed monochrome footage with occasional warm skin tone retained.
- Subtle grain and metallic reflections for texture.
- Fast, restrained motion: text reveal, ticker, image mask and CTA sheen.

Do not make every element animate. The site should feel controlled, not busy.

### Image treatment

Use Lydia as the face of the offer, but prioritise images that show competence: camera in hand, directing a scene, reviewing footage, speaking to a client and working on location. Lifestyle images belong in the founder story and social-proof moments, not as the main evidence.

## 6. Waitlist mechanics

### Form fields

Keep the first step to:

- First name
- Email
- Current role: aspiring marketer / creator / social media manager / business owner / other

Add hidden source and campaign fields. Do not ask for a phone number on the landing page.

### Confirmation state

After sign-up, send users to a proper confirmation view with:

1. A clear “you’re on the list” message.
2. A prompt to check and confirm their email if double opt-in is used.
3. One optional segmentation question about their biggest challenge.
4. An Instagram follow link.

### Waitlist incentive

Offer first access and a genuine founding-member benefit. Best options:

- Founding price held for 48 hours.
- A live launch workshop.
- A practical content-planning template.
- Early access to a limited beta cohort.

Avoid manufactured scarcity unless the cohort is genuinely limited.

### Email sequence

1. Confirmation + what happens next.
2. Lydia's story and why the programme exists.
3. A useful lesson: content creation vs content marketing.
4. Client case study with process and result.
5. Programme reveal and who it is for.
6. Early-access invitation when doors open.

## 7. Required assets before design

- One strong horizontal portrait and two vertical portraits.
- Ten to fifteen short B-roll clips showing real work.
- Three permissioned client cases with objectives, deliverables and outcomes.
- Exact evidence behind the 500K+ reach claim.
- Five short testimonials, preferably outcome-led.
- Client/brand logos with written permission.
- Final audience definition and promise.
- Course format: self-paced, cohort, coaching, community or a combination.
- Founding-member offer and expected launch date.
- Privacy policy and consent copy.

## 8. Build plan

### Phase 1: lock the offer

- Interview Lydia for 60–90 minutes.
- Choose the primary audience and one promised transformation.
- Name the programme and Lydia's method.
- Define course pillars, delivery model and waitlist benefit.
- Audit proof and request permissions.

### Phase 2: copy and wireframe

- Write the full page around objections, transformation and proof.
- Produce desktop and mobile wireframes.
- Prototype the hero, proof strip and waitlist interaction first.
- Review the narrative before spending time on polish.

### Phase 3: visual design

- Create one dark editorial direction with a controlled chrome-gold system.
- Build type, button, form, card and media components.
- Test contrast and legibility; warm bone should carry most light text.
- Create mobile compositions deliberately rather than stacking desktop blocks.

### Phase 4: implementation

- Build the responsive single page.
- Connect the form to the chosen email platform.
- Add validation, double opt-in if required, success state and error handling.
- Add SEO metadata, social sharing image, favicon and privacy links.
- Optimise portraits and video for fast loading.

### Phase 5: measurement and launch

Track:

- `waitlist_view`
- `waitlist_start`
- `waitlist_submit`
- `waitlist_confirmed`
- CTA location
- traffic source and campaign

Primary conversion metric: confirmed waitlist sign-ups divided by unique landing-page visitors. Secondary metrics: hero CTA click rate, form completion rate and confirmation rate.

Test headline and proof order first. Do not begin with cosmetic colour tests.

## 9. Decisions still needed

1. Is the primary buyer an aspiring/freelance marketer, or a business owner marketing their own company? The recommended direction is the first.
2. Is this a recorded course, a cohort, a certification or a coaching/community programme? The page must not imply support that will not exist.
3. What is the programme's single measurable promise? “Learn marketing” is not sufficient.
4. Which client results and logos can Lydia legally and confidently use?
5. What real benefit will early waitlist members receive?

## 10. Guardrails

- Do not copy Shelby Sapp's pink/feminine visual identity; borrow the identity-led transformation, founder authority, community and proof structure.
- Do not overclaim income, job placement, reach or guaranteed results.
- Do not make the page feel like a generic luxury coaching template.
- Do not lead with a long module list.
- Do not make Lydia's lifestyle the only proof of expertise.
- Do not let the black palette erase hierarchy; warm bone, graphite, imagery and metallic detail must create depth.
